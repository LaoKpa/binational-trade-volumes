Understanding Binational Trade Volumes

Using machine learning to explore trends in binational trade and mobility between Mexico and the US.

A capstone project for the Deloitte Consulting Machine Learning Guild

About the project

International commerce can generate a variety of favorable social, economic, and political externalities. In addition to mutual economic gain, trade can also encourage a strengthened social and political bilateral relationship between trading partners.

Recognizing that international trade can often also trigger unfavorable circumstances, this study will focus on better understanding and optimizing the favorable components of a binational commerce relationship. This moves forward under the assumption that the broader economic system will continue to be globalized and so finding ways to optimize the positive externalities is a worthwhile effort for improved political, social and economic dynamics around the world.

Mexico and the United States make ideal selections for this study given the intricate historical trade relationship and the interwoven dynamic in the areas of culture, business and politics. For comparative purposes, trade and mobility data is included for the US-Canadian relationship.

This capstone project aims to answer the following key questions through exploratory data analysis, data preprocessing and regression.

What are we trying to understand?

  • Trade volumes are often a way to quantify a bilateral relationship. If mobility between countries can be a catalyst for a strengthed bilateral relationship (and trade volumes an indicator of an existing relationship), can key datapoints around trade volumes provide any insight into mobility trends? In other words, is there a relationship between mobility data and binational trade data?
  • Can insights in trade and mobility trends support the institutions that facilitate efficiency in both areas?
  • How can better understanding the nuances in the trade data (aggregate import and export volumes) and mobility data provide greater insight into the results of binational commerce?
  • Can binational travel, that may or may not come as a requirement of binational transactions, also be an indictator strengthened cultural mutual understanding?

Ready? Let's go!

About the datasets:

Dataset Source Key Variables
1) General Exports from US to Mexico US International Trade Commission (USITC) Domestic Exports: Aggregate measure of goods grown, produced, or manufactured in the U.S. This includes commodities of foreign origin that have been changed in the US
FAS Export Value: The Free Alongside Ship value is the value of the exports at the U.S. port based on the transaction price
Observances: Aggregate value between 1996-2019
2) General Imports from Mexico to the US US International Trade Commission (USITC) General Imports: The value of the total physical arrivals of merchandise from foreign countries
General Customs Value: The price actually paid or payable for the merchandise excluding U.S. import duties, freight, insurance and other charges
Observances: Aggregate value between 1996-2019
3) US-Mex Border Ports of Entry U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) Port Name: Lists 27 unique ports of entry along the US Mexican Border. See EDA for the complete list.
Measure: Describes the way in which the travel was conducted. There are 12 unique values including: trucks, bus passengers, pedestrians
Value: The recorded number of individuals who traveled through the port name via the measure
Observances: 353,962 mobility records for US-MX and US-Can border between 1996-2019

Disclaimer: The original intent was to quantify the most comprehensive snapshot of total binational travel. This includes airport ports of entry in the United States as well as the value of those travelers that visit Mexico from the United States on an annual aggregate level. Unfortunately, reliable data on these data points was not readily available. This project may be updated with these additional datapoints, if available. Stay tuned and check back periodically!
